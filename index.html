Recon That night they gathered in the shadows across from the mercenaries' house and waited. The house was dark and apparently empty — the men had gone to the Grey Goose in the loud fractious way that Mrs. Harin had described, their departure audible from the far end of the street. Fin counted seven leaving. He waited twenty minutes after the last one before he moved. Earskin tried the knob while Fin produced the ivory lockpick case. Locked, predictably. Fin selected two tools, worked the mechanism with the focused patience of something he'd done often enough that his hands did it while his mind was elsewhere, and felt the pins give. The door opened soundlessly. Inside: the smell of unwashed bodies and old whale oil and, underneath both, the specific metallic quality of cavorite components that had been worked recently. Crates stacked to the ceiling. Earskin moved to the nearest one marked Henson and pried the lid. The ornithopter's tail assembly gleamed inside, silver and precise and entirely real. He lowered the lid. His jaw was set. Fin signalled. Upstairs. He put Earskin in the crawlspace off the guest room. Melanie behind a toppled wardrobe in the far bedroom. Brint in the upstairs closet. Then he looked at Annette. "With me," he said. "Under the stairs." She followed him to the cupboard beneath the staircase. It was small — the kind of space that had been built for coats and forgotten purposes, smelling of dust and old wood and something floral from whatever the previous occupants had stored here. They settled into it and Fin pulled the door to, leaving it open a crack for light and air. Darkness. The sound of the house around them. Outside, distantly, the noise of the street. Annette's shoulder was against his arm. He was aware of this with the same involuntary precision he'd been aware of her hand in Merope, her quill on the airbus, the warmth of her in the space between them at every point in the last several weeks where they'd been closer than professional distance required and neither of them had moved away. "Fin," she said, very quietly. "Hmm." "Why are you in here with me? You could be in the crawlspace. Better sight lines." He didn't answer immediately. The honest answer was that he'd put Earskin in the crawlspace because Earskin was the better tactical choice for it and himself in here because he was not going to put Annette alone in a dark cupboard in a house full of mercenaries — and that this decision had nothing to do with professional judgment and everything to do with something he'd been not-examining for six weeks. "Better angles from here," he said. She turned her head toward him in the dark. He couldn't see her face clearly but he knew the expression — the one that said she was deciding whether to accept the deflection or dismantle it. "Fin." "Annette." "We've been in more dangerous situations than this one," she said. "You haven't stationed yourself next to me in any of them." The house settled around them. A beam somewhere above creaked and went quiet. He exhaled slowly through his nose. He thought about the Henson AC at six thousand feet, the only place Earskin felt fully himself. He thought about Annette writing to Farron on the airbus while he lay there listening to her quill and pretending to sleep and not sleeping at all. He thought about her hand on the anchor chain over the dark water at Andaire. He thought about her in the doorway of the Governor's suite with her derringer already out, entirely steady, the woman she'd decided to be so completely inhabiting the space around her that there wasn't room for doubt. He thought: I have been drifting my whole adult life. I have been a blade in the dark. I have been a man who specifically and deliberately did not dock anywhere, because docking meant loss, and loss was the one thing I had decided not to be available for. He thought: I have apparently made a different decision somewhere in the last six weeks without being consulted about it. "Annette," he said. His voice came out quieter than he intended. "I think I've — become rather attached to you." A silence. Not the silence of surprise exactly. Something more careful. "Rather attached," she said. "I'm aware that's inadequate." "It is somewhat inadequate, yes." "I'm—" He stopped. Started again. "I don't drift toward things. I'm not — built for it, I thought. I spent a long time thinking I wasn't built for it." He paused. "I appear to have been wrong." Another silence. Longer. He was aware of her breathing beside him — steady, slightly slower than usual, the controlled quality of someone choosing their words from a larger set. "Fin." Her voice was careful in the way it got when she meant everything she said. "I have been — aware of you. Since the airbus. In a way that has been — inconvenient, given that we were working." A pause. "I don't make decisions quickly. About things that matter. I've made a great many decisions too quickly in my life and been offered the wrong futures for them. I'm not — I need—" "You don't have to—" "I'm not finished," she said, with a quiet precision that stopped him entirely. "I need you to understand that the fact that I haven't said anything isn't the same as not feeling it. It means I felt it too much to be careless with it." She was quiet for a moment. "Does that make sense?" He thought about a woman who had been heading to sign an engagement contract she felt nothing for and had stepped off the path at the first real invitation to do otherwise. He thought about what that kind of carefulness costs, and what it's worth, and what it means that she was being this careful now. "Yes," he said. "It makes sense." They sat in the dark, her shoulder against his arm, the house quiet around them. "So," Annette said, after a moment. "So," Fin agreed. Neither of them said anything else, because there wasn't anything else to say yet — the thing between them had been named and was now present in the space and would need to be dealt with properly, in light, with time, when they weren't in a dark cupboard waiting to ambush mercenaries. But it had been named. And the naming of it had changed something in the architecture of the air between them, something structural, something that didn't change back. Then the outer door opened. Boots. Voices. The mercenaries were back. Fin pressed a finger to her lips — a brief, entirely practical gesture. She nodded against it. He checked his cogwatch in the dark, the faint matchlight glow: twenty minutes. Long enough for them to settle, put down their weapons, drop their guard. He counted the footsteps. Seven. All of them. Annette's hand found his wrist in the dark — not clinging, just present, a point of contact. He let it stay. Eighteen minutes. Nineteen. He nodded. She released his wrist. They eased out of the cupboard in silence. In the darkened front room, two mercenaries sprawled on cots, boots still on, weapons on the side table within arm's reach. Fin crouched by the nearest, manacles in hand. Annette took the second. He counted down on his fingers — Three. Two. One. The snap of metal was very loud in the silence. The first mercenary came awake with a sound of confusion that ended when Fin's elbow connected with his temple. Silence again. Annette had the second one's wrists locked before he'd fully registered what was happening. She looked across at Fin with the expression of someone who had just done something for the first time and found it fitted. From upstairs — Brint's voice, a sharp shout, then a gunshot. The sound arrived half a second before the pain did. Fin was already moving toward the stairs when his left arm stopped working properly. He looked down. His sleeve was dark and getting darker. He registered this with the strange calm of someone whose body is reporting an event his mind hasn't caught up with yet, took two more steps, and sat down on the bottom stair because his legs had made a unilateral decision. "Fin." Annette was there. Her hands on his arm, pressure immediate and sure, her face very close to his in the dim light. The composure she wore like armour had a crack in it — not gone, just fractured, the warmth underneath it visible in a way it hadn't been before, or hadn't been this visible. "Fin. Look at me." He looked at her. "It's through the muscle," she said. "You're alright." "I know," he said. His voice was steadier than he felt. "You don't have to—" "I know I don't have to," she said, with a precise fierceness that had nothing professional about it at all. She pressed harder on the wound. He winced. She didn't apologise, which was correct, and didn't look away, which was also correct, and her hands were completely steady, which was either courage or training or both, and he thought: this is what it looks like when Annette Elliston is frightened and refusing to be. Earskin came down the stairs with the last mercenary's gun in his hand and stopped when he saw them. He looked at Fin's arm, at Annette's hands, at whatever was on both their faces. He looked away with the tact of someone who understood he'd walked into something that wasn't his. "Animal doctor," he said. "I'll find one." "He's the only kind available at this hour," Brint called from upstairs. "He'll do," Annette said, without looking up. Earskin went. The house was quiet except for the groaning of the one mercenary Brint hadn't fully incapacitated and the sound of Annette's controlled breathing and Fin's less controlled breathing and the particular silence of two people who have said something true in a dark cupboard and are now on either side of an injury deciding what comes next. "Annette," Fin said. "Don't talk. You'll need the energy for the veterinarian." "I just want to say—" "Later," she said. Firmly. Gently. The combination that was entirely hers. "You can say it later. When we're not in a house full of mercenaries and you're not bleeding on the floor." He closed his eyes. He thought: later. He thought: yes. Later. He thought: I appear to have found a shore after all.
